The low sun drapes itself across uneven fields, gilding half‑finished fences and soil still warm from the plough as hands move with quiet urgency, gathering wood, shaping clay, urging reluctant earth to yield before the season slips away. Your family waits with hunger and hope intertwined, their future resting on each small choice made in the dirt. Buried in this hard labour, there is a deep, steady satisfaction, a sense of watching something humble grow into something living, something that belongs to you alone, something shaped by care and intention, something you created as the Agricola by Uwe Rosenberg from Lookout Spiele with art by Klemens Franz.

Life in Agricola is extremely tough. Not only that, but it’s also limited, which may sound harsh, but everything dies eventually. Yes, it all sounds very bleak, but then we’re dealing with a classic Euro game, which, weirdly enough, is actually illustrated relatively colourfully. Originally released in 2007 and revised in 2016, Agricola is one of those titles that helped define what many players now expect from the genre. It is also widely seen as the game that established Uwe Rosenberg as a board game designer. The game became one of his most recognised and influential works.

Agricola is a true worker placement game, a mechanism that leads to plenty of direct player interaction and requires everyone to focus and play tactically, keeping an eye on what other people around the table are doing and anticipating what their next actions may be. Action spaces are limited. They represent important choices that carry a lot of weight. Taking wood early allows for fencing later. However, that decision could lead to another player placing their worker on the highly important food space. Competition for these limited spaces is fierce, sometimes painfully so, which gives the game its distinctly sharp edge.

Luckily, every round, a new action space is revealed. At the same time, every round brings you closer to having to feed your family, as well as to the end of the game, which is played over a fixed number of rounds. So the delight of being able to do more is quickly tempered by the pressure of having to provide for your loved ones and the painful realisation that there is still a lot to be done before the game ends.

Another aspect of Agricola is its scoring system. Unlike many other games, you don’t simply score a certain number of points for each resource or other achievement. Instead, fields, pastures, grain, vegetables, sheep, wild boar, cattle, and other elements are scored according to specific thresholds. For example, you will score a negative one point if you have no or only a single field, one point for two fields, two points for three fields, three points for four and four points for five fields. However, beyond five fields, you don’t score anything extra. So even if you manage to plough ten fields, you still only get four points for them.

That does two things. First of all, negative points are a thing. Everyone starts with a negative balance and works their way up, which emphasises the tough life of the farmer. It also means that overproducing something is a waste of effort. Your goal is to create a balanced farm, which provides just enough of everything to survive. You’re not trying to take extra produce to market for profit. You’re merely trying to subsist.

Now, while the scoring system fits well with the setting, I find it frustrating, especially in the last few rounds. You want to quickly figure out which action will give you the most points, but you have to refer back to the score chart to do so. That doesn’t just slow things down, but also feels annoying. Additionally, scores tend to be tight. Every point counts and can be the difference between victory and loss. While I don’t normally worry about this too much, for such a competitive game as Agricola, winning matters to me a lot more. The game has turned winning into a life-or-death situation for me.

I think it is important to say that I only ever played Agricola online, which means I can’t comment on component quality or table presence. All I can talk about is the underlying design. I can also talk about the online tutorial, which made the game easy to learn, despite there being so many moving parts. Additionally, the game allows players to begin with a simpler version that excludes cards, then gradually add them later for a deeper and more varied gameplay experience, which allows new players to engage with the core systems before exploring more complex options.

Game length also feels good, for what it offers. You really get a feel of building up your smallholding from humble beginnings in a relatively short amount of time. The fixed round limit helps players focus on what’s important and how much time they have left to carry out vital actions. I would say you can finish a game of Agricola within an hour, if you know the game well and play at a good pace, without it feeling rushed.

Agricola remains a classic example of a Euro-style board game. Its combination of clear progression, meaningful choices, and constant pressure creates a game that rewards careful planning and adaptability. Players who enjoy tight decision-making and long-term optimisation will find it especially rewarding. I certainly will return to it again and again. After all, I love growing crops and looking after animals. I enjoy the beautiful countryside, despite the heavy burdens and high pressure that come with looking after a growing family and smallholding.
